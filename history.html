Technology History & Future Trends

The Long
Signal

"Every 'sudden' breakthrough has a forty-year backstory — learn to read it."

Why this matters right now AI, quantum computing, and biotech all feel like overnight arrivals — but each is the visible tip of a decades-long research thread. Understanding how technologies actually move from lab to lab to mainstream is the single best tool for telling real breakthroughs apart from hype, and for predicting what's coming next instead of just reacting to it.
Field Ledger — Selected Entries
1936Turing formalizes computation, decades before a usable computer exists.
1969ARPANET sends its first message — the internet's quiet, unglamorous birth.
1991The web is opened to the public after years inside research labs.
2012A neural net wins an image contest — the deep learning era quietly begins.
PATTERN: the "sudden" moment is always the last step of a much longer chain.
Session lineup

Beginner-friendly sessions

No history or CS background needed — these sessions teach a way of thinking about technology, not a list of dates to memorize.

01

Why Technologies Take 30 Years to "Suddenly" Arrive

A framework for spotting the slow-build phase of a technology — research, prototype, niche, mainstream — and where today's tools actually sit.

02

The Internet Before the Browser

How email, file-sharing, and bulletin boards quietly built the internet's culture before most people had ever seen a web page.

03

Hype Cycles: How to Spot One While You're Inside It

A practical model for separating genuine inflection points from temporary hype — using past examples as a comparison tool.

04

Forgotten Forks: Technologies That Almost Won

Competing standards and products that lost the race — and what their failure teaches about adoption, not just invention.

05

Forecasting Without a Crystal Ball

Simple, repeatable methods for making grounded predictions about where a technology trend goes next.

Hands-on workshop

Tech Archaeology: Build a Timeline From Scratch

Teams receive a technology most people think "appeared overnight" (e.g. smartphones, GPS, voice assistants) and a stack of unlabeled milestone cards spanning decades. Working only from clues on each card, teams reconstruct the correct chronological order, then identify the actual inflection point — and defend their choice to the room.

01 Receive your technology and a shuffled deck of milestone cards
02 Reconstruct the true timeline as a team (15 min)
03 Mark the single moment you believe was the real inflection point
04 Present your timeline and defend your inflection-point pick
